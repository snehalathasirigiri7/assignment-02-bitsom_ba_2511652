
## Database Recommendation
When evaluating MySQL versus MongoDB for a healthcare patient management system, the fundamental considerations revolve around data consistency, transactional guarantees, and regulatory expectations. Healthcare data—patient records, medical histories, prescriptions, diagnostic reports—requires strict accuracy and consistency. These workloads align closely with ACID properties provided by relational database systems like MySQL. ACID ensures that every transaction is processed reliably and completely, which is essential when incorrect or partial data could lead to medical errors, legal issues, or violations of compliance standards such as HIPAA.
MySQL also excels in structured schemas where relationships between entities (patients, doctors, visits, prescriptions) are well defined. Its strong consistency model follows the CP side of the CAP theorem, prioritizing consistency over availability in the presence of partitions—an appropriate choice when correctness is more critical than uptime.
On the other hand, MongoDB operates under the BASE philosophy and provides more flexible schemas suitable for rapidly changing or semi‑structured data. While modern MongoDB supports multi‑document ACID transactions, its core strengths remain scalability, schema flexibility, and availability, aligning more with the AP/CP aspects of the CAP theorem depending on configuration. For a mission‑critical healthcare system, the looser consistency guarantees of BASE may not be ideal.
However, if the system later requires a fraud detection module, the recommendation becomes more nuanced. Fraud detection often benefits from high‑volume ingestion, flexible data models, and large‑scale analytics across heterogeneous signals. In such a case, a hybrid architecture is ideal: MySQL for core patient records where correctness is non‑negotiable, and MongoDB (or another NoSQL store) for fraud‑related event streams and pattern analysis. Thus, while MySQL remains the primary recommendation for patient management, adding MongoDB as a complementary datastore would strengthen fraud detection capabilities without compromising data integrity.
