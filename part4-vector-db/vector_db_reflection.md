## Vector DB Use Case
Traditional keyword-based search systems are limited when dealing with lengthy, complex legal documents such as 500-page contracts. These systems match exact words or phrases and therefore fail when users phrase questions differently from how terms appear in the document. For example, a lawyer asking, “What are the termination clauses?” may not retrieve results if the contract uses the phrase “agreement dissolution conditions” or “end-of-contract provisions.” Keyword search cannot capture semantic meaning, paraphrasing, or contextual nuances. It struggles especially in legal domains where terminology varies widely across agreements and clauses are often written in dense, formal language.
A vector database, however, addresses these limitations by representing text as embeddings—numerical vectors that encode semantic meaning. This allows the system to understand that “termination clause,” “contract end conditions,” and “grounds for agreement cessation” are conceptually related even when the exact words differ. By storing embeddings for contract sections, paragraphs, or sentences, a vector database enables semantic search, retrieving passages that convey the same meaning as the query rather than matching keywords.
In this system, the vector database would serve as the retrieval backbone: it would index contract text as embeddings and return the most semantically relevant sections when a lawyer asks a natural-language question. This dramatically improves discoverability of important clauses, reduces manual review time, and enhances accuracy. Therefore, a keyword search alone is insufficient, while a vector database provides the precise, meaning‑aware retrieval required for legal document intelligence.
